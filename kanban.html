A method for visualizing work and managing flow.

What is Kanban?

Kanban is a method to manage and improve work across human systems. This approach aims to manage work by balancing demands with available capacity, and by improving the handling of system-level bottlenecks.

The core idea is to **visualize the workflow**, **limit Work in Progress (WIP)**, and **manage the flow** of work. It is an evolutionary method that promotes gradual improvement.

The Four Core Principles

Kanban is built on a foundation of four principles to manage and improve the flow of value.

1. Start With What You Do Now

Kanban does not require radical change. It is designed to be applied directly to your current workflow, respecting existing roles and processes.

2. Agree to Pursue Incremental Change

The team agrees that large-scale, radical changes are discouraged as they are often disruptive. Instead, the focus is on small, continuous improvements.

3. Respect Current Process & Roles

Unlike other methods, Kanban does not prescribe specific roles or processes. It recognizes that your current setup has value and seeks to improve it, not replace it.

4. Encourage Leadership at All Levels

Kanban encourages continuous improvement from everyone in the organization, from individual contributors to senior management.

The Six Core Practices

These are the practical, actionable practices required to implement the Kanban method.

1. Visualize the Workflow

This is the **Kanban Board**. By creating a visual model of your work and workflow, you can observe the flow of work and identify problems.

2. Limit Work in Progress (WIP)

This is the cornerstone of Kanban. By limiting how many items can be in progress at once, you reduce multitasking, prevent bottlenecks, and encourage a \"pull\" system.

3. Manage Flow

The goal is to create a smooth, healthy flow of work. Instead of managing people, you manage the work as it moves through the system, monitoring for blockers.

4. Make Policies Explicit

You cannot improve what you don't understand. Make the rules explicit (e.g., \"What does 'Done' mean for this column?\").

5. Implement Feedback Loops

Feedback is essential for improvement. Examples include daily stand-up meetings, replenishment meetings, and service delivery reviews.

6. Improve Collaboratively

Using the visual board and data from the flow, the team collaborates to identify and implement improvements to their process.
